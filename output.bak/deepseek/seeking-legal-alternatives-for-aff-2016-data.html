Excellent question. This gets to the very heart of cyberpunk worldbuilding—the idea of a digital "underworld" or "back alley" that exists parallel to the clean, indexed web we all know.

For a cyberpunk character, finding files that are no longer on the "clearnet" (the standard, indexed web) is a core skill. Here’s a breakdown of where they would look, moving from the more common to the more obscure and illicit.

The Digital Underworld: A Cyberpunk's Toolkit

1. The Usual Suspects (The "Dark Net" Proper)

This is the classic starting point, often accessed via the Tor Browser.

  • Dark Web Search Engines: Unlike Google, these are clunky, unreliable, and full of dead ends—which is perfect for your story. A character might spend hours refining queries on engines like Ahmia or DuckDuckGo's Tor service to find a specific, forgotten forum or repository.
  • Private Forums & Marketplaces: The real treasure isn't the public-facing sites, but the invitation-only forums that are discussed there. Gaining entry requires a reputation, a referral from a trusted source, or sometimes, a "proof of work" like hacking a minor target. These are the digital speakeasies where links to raw data dumps are traded.
  • Wiki-style Directories: Sites like The Hidden Wiki exist, but they are notoriously littered with scams and dead links. A seasoned cyberpunk would know that the real directories are passed person-to-person on encrypted messaging apps.

2. The Archives & Digital Graveyards

This is where deleted clearnet content goes to die—or be resurrected.

  • The Internet Archive (Archive.org): The most legitimate tool on this list, but a goldmine for a researcher. Your character could find old versions of websites, before certain files were "scrubbed." The tension comes from seeing a file listed in an old index, but finding the specific snapshot is missing or corrupted—a perfect plot hook.
  • Archive.today / Archive.is: A more modern, snapshot-based archive. Useful for capturing a single page at a specific moment in time.
  • Specialized Data Archives: For your "AFF 2016" example, a character might look for sites like Cyberlockers or Textboard archives that are notorious for hoarding everything, from public data breaches to scanned corporate manuals. Finding the right one is the challenge.

3. The Peer-to-Peer (P2P) Underworld

This is the decentralized, resilient network that is very hard to kill.

  • Decentralized Networks (Freenet, ZeroNet, IPFS): These are pure cyberpunk fodder.

* Freenet: A distributed, anonymous data store. Files are stored in chunks across the network. There is no central server to take down. Finding something requires a specific, complex key—a "Freenet Key"—which is like a digital treasure map. IPFS (InterPlanetary File System): A file is located by what it is (a cryptographic hash), not where it is*. If your character has the correct hash (e.g., QmXoypizjW3WknFiJnKLwHCnL72vedxjQkDDP1mXWo6uco), they can pull the file from anyone else on the network who has it, even if the original source is gone. Losing the hash means the file is almost certainly lost forever.

  • File-Sharing Networks (BitTorrent): Specifically, they would be searching for and seeding old, niche torrents with zero peers. The drama of "connecting to a single seed in a forgotten corner of the world" is a classic cyberpunk trope.

4. The Human Network

The most secure method is to go offline.

  • Encrypted Messaging Apps: The real trade happens in private channels on Signal, Telegram, Keybase, or Session. A character would have to know the right person to ask, and that interaction could involve a trade, a favor, or a currency transfer (like Monero).
  • IRC (Internet Relay Chat): The ancient, text-based protocol that never truly died. It's where the old guard and the purists hang out. Finding the right server and channel (#data-hoarders, #lost-media) is a skill in itself.

How a Cyberpunk Scene Might Unfold:

Your protagonist, let's call her Jax, needs the "AFF 2016" schematic.

  1. The Brush-Off: She starts on a dark web search engine. It's slow. The first ten links are phishing traps or malware. The eleventh leads to a defunct forum.
  2. The Lead: On a cached version of that forum from Archive.org, she finds a user mentioning a "ZeroNet soup" (a ZeroNet site) dedicated to "obsolete architectures."
  3. The Gate: She fires up ZeroNet. The site is there, but it's a mess of broken links and incomplete files. In a comment section, she finds a cryptic string: a Freenet key.
  4. The Lair: She connects to Freenet. The key unlocks a massive, well-organized repository of files. But the "AFF 2016" folder is empty except for a readme.txt file.
  5. The Human Element: The readme contains a Signal number and a passphrase. She makes contact. The person on the other end, a data-hoarder named "Sphinx," doesn't want money. He wants a piece of data that Jax has—a fragment of code from a rival corporation. A trade is negotiated in terse, encrypted messages.
  6. The Payoff: The trade is made. The file is transferred peer-to-peer. Jax has her schematic, but now she owes Sphinx a favor, and her digital footprint has grown, leaving a trail for antagonists to follow.

This layered approach—moving through different technologies and requiring both technical skill and social engineering—is what makes the search for digital ghosts feel authentic and compelling in a cyberpunk narrative.
