In Domain-Driven Design, a Saga is a pattern for managing long-running business processes that span multiple aggregates or services, where you need to maintain data consistency without relying on distributed transactions.

The Core Idea

When a business process involves multiple steps across different aggregates (or microservices), you can't use a traditional ACID transaction. A saga breaks the process into a sequence of local transactions, each publishing events that trigger the next step.

Two Main Approaches

Choreography: Each service listens for events and decides what to do next. There's no central coordinator—services react to events independently.

Orchestration: A central saga orchestrator explicitly tells each participant what to do and tracks the overall state of the process.

Compensating Transactions

Since you can't rollback across services, sagas use compensating transactions—actions that semantically undo previous steps if something fails. For example, if step 3 fails, you'd run compensations for steps 2 and 1 in reverse order.

A Classic Example

An order process saga might look like:

  1. Create order (pending)
  2. Reserve inventory
  3. Process payment
  4. Confirm order

If payment fails, the saga would compensate by releasing the reserved inventory and cancelling the order.

Key Distinction

Sagas are sometimes confused with process managers (which route messages and make decisions) or domain events (which are just facts about what happened). A saga specifically handles the coordination and compensation logic for multi-step processes.
