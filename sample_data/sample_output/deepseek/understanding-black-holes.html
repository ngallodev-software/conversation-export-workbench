A black hole is a region of space where gravity is so intense that nothing — not even light — can escape once it crosses a boundary called the event horizon.

How do black holes form?

Most black holes form when a massive star (more than ~20× the mass of the Sun) runs out of nuclear fuel and collapses under its own gravity. The core compresses into an incredibly dense point called a singularity.

Why can't light escape?

The key concept is escape velocity — the speed you need to break free from a gravitational field.

  • To escape Earth, you need to travel ~11 km/s.
  • Near a black hole, the escape velocity exceeds the speed of light (299,792 km/s).
  • Since nothing travels faster than light, nothing gets out.

The radius at which escape velocity equals the speed of light is called the Schwarzschild radius. For the Sun, it would be about 3 km — crush the entire Sun into a sphere 3 km across and you'd have a black hole.

The event horizon

The event horizon isn't a physical surface — it's just the "point of no return." If you were falling in, you wouldn't feel anything special as you crossed it. But from the outside, you'd appear frozen in time due to extreme gravitational time dilation (a prediction of general relativity).

You heard right! Hawking radiation is a fascinating theoretical prediction by physicist Stephen Hawking (1974).

The quantum trick

Quantum mechanics tells us that even "empty" space is filled with pairs of virtual particles constantly popping into and out of existence. Normally they annihilate each other immediately.

Near the event horizon, something strange happens:

  1. A particle pair forms right at the edge.
  2. One particle falls into the black hole.
  3. The other escapes as real radiation.

The black hole effectively loses a tiny bit of mass to compensate — it "pays" for the escaping particle.

Implications

  • Black holes very slowly evaporate over astronomical timescales.
  • A stellar-mass black hole would take ~10⁶⁷ years to evaporate — far longer than the current age of the universe.
  • Tiny primordial black holes (if they exist) could be evaporating right now.

The information paradox

Hawking radiation is essentially thermal — it carries no information about what fell in. This creates a famous unsolved problem: if a black hole evaporates completely, what happened to all the information about the matter that formed it? This "black hole information paradox" is still actively debated in theoretical physics.

Note: Hawking radiation has never been directly observed — the signal from a stellar-mass black hole is far too faint to detect with current technology. It remains a theoretical prediction.
