Photosynthesis is the process plants use to make their own food. Here's the simplified version:

The basic idea

Plants capture sunlight and use it to convert carbon dioxide (CO₂) from the air and water from the soil into glucose (sugar) and oxygen.

The equation

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Where it happens

This process takes place inside chloroplasts — tiny green structures in plant cells. Chloroplasts contain a pigment called chlorophyll, which gives plants their green color and is what actually absorbs sunlight.

Two main stages

  1. Light-dependent reactions — happen in the thylakoid membranes, use sunlight to split water and produce ATP (energy currency).
  2. Calvin cycle — happens in the stroma, uses that ATP to build glucose from CO₂.

In short: plants eat sunlight and breathe out oxygen. The glucose they produce fuels their growth and everything they do.

Great question! Doubling atmospheric CO₂ would have a notable but nuanced effect on photosynthesis:

Short-term boost (CO₂ fertilization)

  • More CO₂ means more raw material for the Calvin cycle, so photosynthesis rates generally increase — up to ~30–50% under ideal conditions.
  • Plants may also partially close their stomata (the pores they breathe through), reducing water loss while maintaining the same carbon uptake.

Why it doesn't keep scaling

Other factors quickly become the bottleneck:

Limiting factorEffect
LightNo extra CO₂ helps if there isn't enough sunlight
WaterDrought negates the stomatal benefit
NutrientsPlants need nitrogen, phosphorus, etc. to grow more
TemperaturePhotosynthetic enzymes (like RuBisCO) have optimal ranges

Real-world complexity

  • Not all plants benefit equally. C3 plants (wheat, rice, trees) gain more than C4 plants (corn, sugarcane), which already concentrate CO₂ internally.
  • Faster growth may dilute the nutritional density of crops (lower protein and mineral content per gram).
  • The overall climate warming caused by higher CO₂ would partially offset gains.

So: a short-term win for photosynthesis, but with significant caveats when the full picture is considered.
